Exploratory Data Analysis of the 2015 Street Tree Census - New York City

On this page, I explore and analyze the "2015 Street Tree Census - Tree Data" database (raw data available here.) This survey counts trees on the streets of New York City and includes data about each tree's species, health, size, location, and surroundings. The census was organized by the New York City Department of Parks and Recreation. It is important to note that only 43% of this data was collected by specifically trained and paid staff for this census. Another 25% was collected by New York City Park staff who were not explictly hired for this task. The remaining 32% was collected by unpaid volunteers. While all participants received some amount of training (the link to the training PDF is unfortunately dead), there is always a possibility of misleading or inaccurately collected data. This is especially true for qualitative variables such as the health of a tree: which category the tree was placed in was still up to the discretion of the person collecting the data, and different people may have had different criteria. Here, I will analyze seven primary variables: tree species, tree health, tree status, diameter of the tree, presence and type of a tree guard, number of signs of stewardship, and the borough the tree is in.

Data Summary

  • There were 683,788 trees recorded in the 2015 Street Tree Census. Of those, 17,654 were stumps and 13,961 were standing dead trees. These totalled 31,616 trees, and because they were dead or stumps, they were not counted in several categories. Specifically, tree health, signs of stewardship, tree species, and presence and types of tree guards were not recorded for dead trees and stumps.
  • Of the 652,173 live trees:
    • 528,850 trees were deemed to be in "good" health, 96,504 trees in "fair" health, and 26,818 trees in "poor" health.
    • 487,823 trees showed no signs of stewardship, 143,557 trees showed one or two signs of stewardship, 19,183 showed three or four signs of stewardships, and 1,610 trees showed four or more signs of stewardship. Despite the apparent overlap between "three or four" and "four or more" signs, no trees were doubled counted in both categories. We can assume that one of the categories was misnamed, or it was up to an indivdual to decide which category to place a tree with four signs of stewardship in, but I could not determine either way.
    • 572,306 trees had no tree guard present. Of the 79,897 trees that had tree guards, 51,866 of them had tree guards that were deemed helpful in protecting the tree, 20,252 trees had tree guards that harmed the tree, and census takers were unsure if the tree guard was helpful or harmful on 7,748 trees.
    • 132 species of tree were counted in this census. I will elaborate on the 10 most common, and group the rest as "other". There were 87,014 London planetrees, 64,264 Honeylocusts, 58,931 Callery pears, 53,185 pin oaks, 34,189 Norway maples, 29,742 littleleaf lindens, 29,279 cherries, 29,258 Japanese zelkovas, 21,024 gingkos, and 19,338 sophoras. The remaining 225,945 trees belonged to various other species.
  • Going back to statistics evaluated for all 683,788 trees, including the dead, standing trees and stumps:
    • 204,497 trees had a diameter between 1 and 5 inches (inclusive, rounded to the nearest whole inch). 160,056 had a diameter beyween 6 and 10 inches, 132,901 between 11 and 15 inches, 79,502 between 16 and 20 inches, 50,732 between 21 and 25 inches, 29,510 between 26 and 30 inches, 16,778 between 31 and 35 inches, and 9,534 with a diameter of 36 inches or greater. Notably, 80 trees were recorded with a diameter of greater than 100 inches, with the maximum value being 450 inches. For reference, that equates to 37 feet in diameter. The world record for largest tree diameter is 36 feet, so this is rather unlikely. According to the NYC Department of Parks and Recreation, diameter measurements for standing trees were taken by measuring circumference and dividing by 3.14. This calculation may not have been performed on some data, causing large outliers, but even then, that would leave a diameter of 143 inches or nearly twelve feet. I will be visualizing the data that I have available, but note that the large numbers are especially unreliable.
    • 250,551 trees were observed in the Queens borough. 177,293 trees were in Brooklyn, 105,318 trees in Staten Island, 85,203 trees in the Bronx, and 65,423 trees in Manhattan.

Hypotheses

  1. Trees with signs of stewardship are generally larger and healthier.

    2. Looking at the figure "Median Diameter of New York City Trees by Number of Signs of Stewardship", it is true that the trees with no signs of stewardship tend to be larger. However, looking at the frequency graph of "Signs of Stewardship in New York City", we see that the majority of trees (just under five hundred thousand) have no signs of stewardship present. Also, large trees may seem much stronger, so they don't visibly need the stewardship a thin, weak tree might. When we look at trees with 1 to 2 signs of stewardship, they tend to be smaller than trees with 3, 4, or more signs of stewardship. The data here is somewhat inconsistent, but it looks like there is a possible connection that can be explored.

  2. Trees guards are more effective in protecting a tree earlier in life.

    3. When examining the "Median Diameter of New York City Trees with Guards, by Borough", we see that in every borough but Staten Island, the trees with harmful tree guards have a larger median diameter. On Staten Island, they have the same median diamter. To me, this suggests that, rather than the tree guard causing the size difference, tree guards on smaller trees are more likely to be seen as helpful. A tree guard is meant to protect a tree from damage, but can in itself hurt the tree if it isn't made larger as the tree grows, or if it stops the tree from getting water. Thus, the cost-benefit is probably higher on thinner trees, which are weak and need protection, while sturdy trees with a large diameter do not need the help, and are more likely to be hurt by the tree guard.

  3. Different boroughs of New York City have different ideas, standards, and regulations around curbside trees.

    4. In the same figure as above, examine the difference in median diameter from trees with a harmful tree guard to trees with a helpful tree guard. This varies quite a bit based on borough. I hypothesize the difference is due to cultural or legal differences between them. For example, the median diameter of trees with both types of tree guard is the same on Staten Island. This could be because either almost all or very few trees have tree guards at all, because the majority of trees on Staten Island were planted at a similar time and grew to a similar diameter, or because their parks and recreation officers are very discerning with when a tree guard is needed. There are many interesting possibilities. While this would be harder to test than the other two hypotheses, it would be possible with more thorough per-borough censuses, or by surveying parks and recreation policies.
